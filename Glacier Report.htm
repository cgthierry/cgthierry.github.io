Abstract:

         Large glaciers formed in the western South Lake Tahoe region and created several distinct landforms including Emerald Bay, Cascade Lake, and Granite Lake. Moraines of Tahoe and Tioga age glaciations are visible alongside these bodies of water and can be used to map the extent of glaciation within this region. Studying this region gives an indication of glacial history, which can be used to understand glacial records around the world.

         Watershed area and sediment volume in Emerald Bay are used to determine interglacial erosion rates and are calculated using a grid overlay on top of the glaciation map. Simultaneously, the accumulation-area ratios are calculated are used to check the equilibrium line altitude. The estimated erosion rate of the Eagle Creek watershed is 8.3*10-6 m/year. The accumulation-area ratio for Cascade Lake and Emerald Bay glaciers was 0.77 and 0.85, respectively. The average ELA value is 2250m for both systems, with an error of  for Cascade Lake and an error of  for Emerald Bay. Basal shear stress calculations are used to determine the location of ice contours and to better understand glacial surface topography. The average basal shear stress in the Cascade Glacier is 1.2 bars.

         The ultimate hope for this research is to reconstruct the surface of the Tioga age glacier, determine the extent of glaciation, calculate the inter-glacial erosion rate, and to detail the effects of faulting on glacial features, which will help to understand glaciation, glacial features and topographical effects.

Introduction:

         In order to understand glaciation in the Sierra Nevada range, one must look at the role of climate in the development of glaciers, as well as the role of faults in the disruption of glacial deposits. The Lake Tahoe Tioga age glaciation was mapped using tools such as, LiDAR, aerial photographs, Google Earth, contour maps, field notes, and external research. A map was created with the intent of showing the extent of glaciation in the Emerald Bay – Cascade Lake region. This map was then used to discuss erosion, landslides, and faulting mechanisms.

         According to Blackwelder (1931), J.D. Whitney realized that the Sierra Nevada range was glaciated in 1865 – making the discovery of Lake Tahoe glaciers a relatively new one. At the time of discovery and later, when Blackwelder mapped these glaciers, dating techniques were primitive and relative. Therefore, Blackwelder utilized four relative stratigraphic orders: Tioga, Tahoe, Sherwin, and McGee. Howle (2012), dates the Tioga age at 20.81.4 ky and Tahoe at 69.24.8ky and 73.28.7ky.

         According to Gibbons et al. (1984), relative dating is more nuanced than the technique used by Blackwelder. Blackwelder looked at the relative extent, amount of weathering, and number of boulders within a glacial deposit. He used relative qualitative data to determine if the deposit belonged to Tioga or Tahoe glaciation. As described in Table 1, Tioga moraines are typically gray, give off a pinging sound, have a greater number of large boulders, and are less weathered than Tahoe moraines. In contrast, Tahoe moraines as red from rust, boulders giving off a dull sound when struck, fewer large boulders, and more weathered than Tioga moraines.  Gibbons et al. (1984) discusses the idea of Òobliterative overlap,Ó in which a moraine is destroyed by a subsequent glacial advance. This means that the relative order of moraine succession is complicated by the potential for hidden or obliterated moraine sequences. This makes relative and absolute dating complicated. It is important to utilize absolute dating so that one can infer whether there is a missing moraine.

         Another complication, as described by Maloney et al. (2013), is the West Tahoe – Dollar Point fault, which could offset moraines through faulting or obliterate moraines through subsequent landslides and tsunamis. In fact, there is evidence of two landslides on the floor of Cascade Lake (Maloney et al, 1081). Moore et al (2014) discusses the possibility of a landslide-generated tsunami destroying Tioga aged moraines around the lake – this also presents an issue with BlackwelderÕs relative dating scheme. Moore also discussed the possibility of the tsunami-generated waters pooling in the south of the lake and potentially causing continuous erosion as the waters drain. This further complicates the glacial history that is seen in the Cascade Lake – Emerald Bay region.

         In addition to Howle (2012)Õs estimates, Phillips et al. (2009), uses cosmogenic dating to determine absolute ages for glacial moraines. Using this technique, Phillips divides the Tioga glaciation into four-subdivisions (I-IV). The absolute dating helps to alleviate uncertainty in BlackwelderÕs glacial stages. Unfortunately, this technique still falls prey to Gibbons et al (1984)Õs Òobliterative overlapÓ. Phillips et al. (2009) is also concerned with moraine erosion in which boulders of older age are exposed due to erosion of unconsolidated material. These boulders might be used for dating and yield an age that is much older than the moraine.

         Despite these issues, these resources act as a sounding board for the results that are developed in this report and assist in the development of the glacial map. In order to complete this map the following techniques are used to check for accuracy: ELA estimation, basal shear stress, and accumulation-area ratio. Pierce (1979) used the same techniques to map and understand the glaciation of Yellowstone National Park. Pierce mentions that some of these methods were developed using New Zealand data and may not be accurate for North American ice caps. This comes to realization in the calculation of the AAR, under normal circumstances the AAR should equal . However, Phillips et al (2009) mentions that the AAR calculation is biased toward mountain-valley glaciers and does not accurately represent glacial systems with alternative area-altitude distributions.

         Another important aspect of this analysis, is considering how location plays into the formation of these glaciers. Rood and Rinkel (2011) used ­­10Be to cosmogenically date the entire Sierra Nevada glacier system. They raise the concern that these glaciers may have been affected by their proximity to the Pacific Ocean and may have been particularly sensitive to precipitation, as well as, air and sea-surface temperature. These sensitivities make the Lake Tahoe glaciers less representative for worldwide glaciation because these glaciers formed faster here than elsewhere.

Methods:

         Prior to surveying the area, the locations of moraines, trim lines, equilibrium lines of altitude, and the extent of glaciation were estimated on a 1:40,000 contour map. These locations were estimated using Google Earth images, and contour line and LiDAR maps. Upon arrival in the field, these locations and extents were refined using observational and qualitative data. These data were collected based off of the appearance and location of boulders, relative extent of weathering, apparent Tahoe-Tioga contacts, as well as, glacial striations and chatter marks. Map units are detailed in Table 1, and were used to differentiate between the three till types (Tahoe, Tioga, and undifferentiated), sediment types, extent of glaciation, fault locations, glacial features including striations, cirques, moraine crests, contacts, and ice divides.

         While in the field, stops 1 through 2.7 looked at a road cut that showed Tioga moraine and Tahoe/Tioga moraines that were affected by faulting. Stop 3 looked at a road cut ridge, which showed a Tahoe-Tioga contact. Stops 3.1 – 3.4 followed the road along the edge of Emerald Bay and gave the opportunity to view the opposing moraine crest and ridgeline. It was from this vantage point that the end elevation of the moraine crest was estimated to be 2075m. At stops 3.3 and 3.4, the volume of sediments deposited in Emerald Bay was estimated to be on the order of 106m3. Stop 3.5 was the last stop along the road and gave the opportunity to measure glacial striation directions (Table 2). Stops 4 – 4.7 were along the trail from Emerald Bay to MaggieÕs Peak and were used to evaluate the trim line along the opposing ridgelines. Stop 4.1 yielded an opportunity to measure striations and chatter marks and to analyze the local glacial flow (Table 2). Stop 4.7 showed overland flow and rill and stop 4.8 allowed for analysis of the Granite Lake cirque. The final stop, stop 5, looked at a sandblasted saprolite road cut, which had large, stacked boulders resting on top.

         Relative-age determination is made more difficult because ages are not comparable between ranges or valleys and assessor bias. There is a Òdown-valleyÓ expectation to find older moraine features, which biases the observer into this expectation (Pierce, 1979). These biases complicate the mapping of this glacial system as well as the relative age ranking of the moraines.

         The accumulation-area ratio was calculated using a grid overlay on top of the glacier map. The boxes within the trim and moraine line were added to create a total area for the glacier. This was done again to find the accumulation area. The accumulation area was subsequently divided by the total area to find the ratio between the two. The same technique was used in estimating the watershed area of Eagle Creek, except the delineation was the ridgeline instead of the trim line.

         The equilibrium line altitudes were calculated for the Cascade Lake and Emerald Bay glaciers in three manners. The first technique using the highest moraine elevation as the ELA, the second using the AAR, and the third using the lowest cirque floor. These three techniques produced values that are detailed in Table 3. The first technique took the highest moraine elevation for each system and assumed that was the ELA. The second technique took the total area, multiplied that by 0.65 to find the accumulation area, and then used that area to determine where the ELA was located. The third technique took the lowest elevation of the Granite Lake Cirque, and assumed that that was the ELA for both glacial systems.

Results:

The accumulation-area ratio for the Cascade Lake and Emerald Bay glaciers is 0.77 and 0.85 respectively. The expected AAR for this region is , however, authors (e.g. Meier and Post, 1962) have written that the actual AAR may be vastly different because of an Òasymmetric area-to-altitude distributionÓ (Pierce, 1979).

            The ELA estimates varied by up to 325 m and produced average values of m (Cascade Lake) and m (Emerald Bay). The ELA estimate using the maximum elevation of the lateral moraines was used in the completion of the glacier map. This was decided based off of the previous explanation of AAR calculations and because the lowest cirque floor so far above the lateral moraines that it seemed unreasonable to estimate the ELA from this.

            The Eagle Creek watershed area was calculated to be 19.2 km2. This differs slightly from the 24-km2 estimate made by Maloney et al (2013). This difference is likely due to varying locations of ice divides. The sediment volume found at the outlet of Eagle Creek was estimated in the field to be 1.6*106 m3. The erosion rate for Eagle Creek is 8.3*10-6 m/year, which is within the same order of magnitude calculated by Rood et al (2011) (3.1*10-6m/year).

            The following stops require further discussion: 4.5, 4.6, 4.8, and 5. Stop 4.5 showed a fresh boulder resting on granular rocks, which could be explained by either Tioga glaciation with rock susceptible to chemical weathering or by this location being the birthplace to glaciers resulting in thin ice with low erosive power. There is no clear evidence that points to either explanation as being better than the other. Stop 4.6 raised the question of whether DickÕs Pass was glaciated and whether it was an ice divide. Based off of topography, contour lines, and elevation, I believe that DickÕs Pass was glaciated and therefore an ice divide. Stop 4.8 questioned what kind of feature Granite Lake was. I believe that this area is a cirque basin and the ridgelines surrounding it are moraines. The missing sister moraine to the north is likely covered and eroded by extensive forestation. The final stop shows jointed granitic bedrock that was fractured by water infiltration and raised by faulting. Further diffusion of water turned the granite into saprolite. The ravine that is just beyond the non-coastal ridge is indicative of normal faulting.

Discussion:

The elevation of the lowest cirque floor was 2275m, which becomes the ELA estimate for Cascade Lake and Emerald Bay. The maximum extent of lateral moraines, as determined by LiDAR imaging and visual inspection in the field, was 2100m for Cascade Lake and 2075m for Emerald Bay. Using ice divides, trim lines, and glacial striations, the accumulation-area ratio was calculated for both Cascade Lake and Emerald Bay and yielded ELA estimates of 2375m for Cascade Lake and 2400m for Emerald Bay. These differences can be explained by each estimation technique. A cirque is an isolated glacial system and due to its proximity must be above the lateral moraines. Therefore, the floor of the Granite Lake cirque must be above the ELA. The accumulation area ratio estimates a large ELA, which is due to glacial movement, errors in ice divide locations and in extent of glaciation. Additionally, Sharp (1963) writes that the Sierra Nevada range was composed of piedmont slopes, which is cited in Pierce (1979) to make the system have wild AAR values.

Considering the difficulties in calculating an accurate equilibrium line altitude and in mapping trim lines, there is a considerable amount of error in the shear stress calculation. The trim lines affect the location of the ice surface contours and the AAR affects the confidence in the ELA location. Both of these factors are directly related to the shear stress calculation, and if either one of these are wrong, then the error in the basal shear stress calculation is considerable.

Erosion processes in the Eagle Creek watershed include relief failure, water runoff through soil and saprolite, biotic stress, freeze-thaw, wind, and stress due to faulting. These erosion processes move soil and rocks from the hill slope into the watershed, then to Emerald Bay, and finally into Lake Tahoe. Weathering during interglacial times due to these processes would free sediments. These sediments could be picked up by the glacier, or be caught in the glacier. The glacier would then move these sediments out to the lateral and terminal moraines and the lake. The volume of sediment measured in Emerald Bay is assumed to be the net erosion into Emerald Bay.

Evidence of faulting was seen along the Tahoe and Tioga moraines between the glacial systems. Faulting and fault fracturing were also seen along the northwestern coast of the lake (stop 5). The fault is a normal fault and causes the eastern plate to dip in respect to the western edge. This is seen as a stepping pattern within the geography of the area. Moore et al (2014) write about the tsunami that occurred more than 12,000 years ago. This tsunami was likely caused by a large landslide, which could have been due to a large earthquake. The last large tsunami in this area caused the displacement of so much water that an area of 30 percent of the lakeÕs size was covered in 100m of water (assuming a uniform distribution of water). This makes the study of these active fault lines pertinent as most of Lake TahoeÕs residents live in South Lake Tahoe – which was potentially decimated by a 7 km wide wave from the Tahoe KeyÕs tributaries during the last tsunami.

            The flow at the base of the glacier is uniform within 14 percent. This actually allows for quite a bit of variation in the flow. According to Pierce (1979), this is likely caused by local topography. For example, a large, strong boulder could force ice to redirect flow and therefore change the direction of the striations.

Conclusion:

            The map and the field observations conclusively find that there are clearly two distinct moraine systems in this region – one of Tioga age and the other of Tahoe age, however without cosmogenic dating it is difficult to see evidence of other glaciations. This glacier system was extensive and destructive. The evidence of faulting indicates that this region has been recently tectonically active and is likely to continue to be so, and finally, the landslide evidence indicates the serious possibility of severe landslides occurring in and decimating this region thus making this region an important one for study.
